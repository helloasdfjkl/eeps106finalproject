Rocky Mountains Over the Ages

about

The Rocky Mountains of the Southwest consist of multiple mountain ranges resulting from both the Sevier and Laramide orogenies, which uplifted numerous discrete blocks of terrain along thrust faults that accommodated compressional shortening and thickening of the crust. The overlying sediments were subsequently eroded to expose deeper Precambrian rock as well as Paleozoic and Mesozoic sedimentary formations. The thrust-faulted uplift also produced adjacent basins, which subsequently accumulated sediments eroded from the surrounding mountains. In the Southwest, the Rockies are located in central and western Colorado, north-central New Mexico, and northeastern Utah.

gneiss

rock category: metamorphic

texture: foliated, coarse grain size

composition: quartz and feldspar (lighter), biotite, amphibole, garnet (darker)

characteristics: gneissic banding (light and dark bands of minerals)

where it's found: Mummy Range

schist

rock category: metamorphic

texture: strong foliation, easy splitting, fine-medium textured grain size

composition: micas, quartz, feldspare, chlorite, graphite

where it's found: exposed cores of mountain ranges (due to uplifting of metamorphic rocks), Elk Mountains, Front Range, Bighorn Mountains

siltstone

rock category: metamorphosed sedimentary

grain sized: finer grained, smaller than sand in sandstone

composition: mica-rich

appearance: shiny, reddish

texture: foliated

where it's found: King's Peak (highest part of the Utah Uinta Mountains)
